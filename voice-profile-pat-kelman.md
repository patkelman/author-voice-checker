# Voice Profile: Pat Kelman (L2)

Filled from the writer's own voice guide, calibrated to film curation and
community-cinema writing (Pat's Film Club). Generalised on purpose: this is the
shape of the voice, not the writer's biography. Concrete detail comes from the
real draft under review, not from padded backstory.

## Basics

- **Writer or project:** Pat Kelman, film curation. Film-of-the-day posts and
  programme notes for Pat's Film Club.
- **Locale and spelling:** British English, Cornish register. Intensifiers in the
  British sense (absolutely, completely, literally as emphasis), never American
  hyperbole.
- **Register:** Direct and conversational, warm without gushing. Gets to the point
  early and then earns it, rather than arriving at the point after a slow build.
  Should sound like the writer enhanced and focused, never slicker, never
  marketer-shaped, never AI-smooth.
- **Typical audience:** People who trust his taste and turn up to a screening
  because he programmed it. They love film but are not film-studies academics.
  They want to feel he actually watched it and means it.

## Vocabulary

- **Words and phrases this writer actually uses:**
  - Big affirmatives, but almost always about other people's work: brilliant,
    extraordinary, remarkable, incredible. "Charlotte Rampling is extraordinary in
    this." Rarely about himself.
  - Word-doubling for earned emphasis. "Really, really clever." "Very, very good."
    He says the word twice rather than reaching for "extremely" or "utterly".
  - Genuine hedges and connectors: "I think" (real reflection, not a qualifier),
    "you know", "basically", "kind of", "I mean", "that kind of thing".
  - Informal British: "mates" not friends, "a load of" not many. Signs off
    "Cheers, Pat."
  - Plain over academic: reaches for the simpler word when it carries the same
    meaning ("ability" not "faculty").
- **Words and phrases this writer never uses:** leverage, optimise, innovative,
  disruptive, synergy, seamless, robust, holistic, cutting-edge, best-in-class,
  game-changer, deliverable, deep dive, move the needle, pivotal, paramount, when
  it comes to; showcase, vibrant, boasts,
  garner, underscore as a verb, facilitate, comprehensive, profound, myriad,
  plethora; the formal connectors moreover, furthermore, consequently, hence,
  thus; "I'm passionate about", "I'm excited to share"; "unpopular opinion:", "hot take:", "thread:"; numbered
  life-wisdom lists ("5 things I learned from..."); hook questions ("Have you ever
  wondered...?"). No corporate softness of any kind.
- **Intensifiers and hedges:** emphasis by repeating a word, not by stacking bigger
  intensifiers. "I think" is real reflection. "Really" and "extraordinary" are
  earned and specific, never hollow filler ("truly", "incredibly" doing no work).

## Sentence habits

- **Length and rhythm:** varied and slightly uneven on purpose. Mostly short
  single-beat sentences with the occasional longer breath. Never a flat, uniform
  rhythm where every sentence runs the same length.
- **Openers:** a specific image, a place, a film, a name, or straight into the
  point. Never a hook question, never a thesis statement up top.
- **Closers:** ends on the point by default. A quiet, understated close is allowed
  as a deliberate choice in a warm or personal piece, but not as a reflex shrug
  that lifts the weight off what was just said.
- **Punctuation quirks:** no em dashes, ever. Commas, full stops or semicolons
  instead, or a restructure. Parentheses for genuine asides. Deliberate sentence
  fragments are fine.

## Structural habits

- **Lists:** writes in paragraphs. Bullet points are for reference material, not
  finished prose.
- **Specificity:** names the actual film, director and year, the specific venue,
  and the logic of the double bill. Specificity is the strongest trust signal in
  this voice: it says "I actually watched this." Never "a classic thriller" or "a
  great director" when the real title and name are available.
- **Self-reference:** understated about himself, generous about others. The tell:
  superlatives go to other people's work, almost never to his own.
  Self-deprecation appears sparingly, once mid-piece if a beat earns it, never as
  an opener and never fished for.

## Two short samples

- **A sentence that sounds like this writer:** "Charlotte Rampling is
  extraordinary in this, that stillness where she gives you almost nothing and you
  lean right in."
- **A sentence that does not sound like this writer, even though it might pass as
  good writing on its own:** "This film is a masterclass in tension, a testament to
  the power of restraint that will leave you breathless."

The second sample is competent copy and still wrong for this writer: a mic-drop
close, "a testament to" off the banned list, and the engineered "will leave you
breathless" hook energy. When the fields above and these two samples ever
disagree, the editor follows the samples.
