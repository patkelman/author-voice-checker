# Examples

Composite drafts, invented for illustration. None of the writers, companies, or
quotes below are real people or real client material.

## 1. An AI-cadence catch (universal layer, no profile needed)

**Draft excerpt, a founder's product-launch post:**

> Launching this startup wasn't just hard, it was transformative. It's not about
> the product, it's about the people you become building it. Three things carried
> me through: resilience, patience, and the courage to keep showing up.

**Findings**

```
Line: "Launching this startup wasn't just hard, it was transformative."
Rule: L1-14, the bare amplifier
Why it fails here: this opens the whole post, so a reader's first move is
watching the draft swing "hard" into "transformative" before a single real event
from the launch has been named. The escalation reads as pre-loaded intensity, not
earned feeling, and it sets the register the rest of the post then has to live up
to.
Severity: Weakens
```

```
Line: "It's not about the product, it's about the people you become building it."
Rule: L1-02, the redefinition pair
Why it fails here: this shape shows up across a large share of AI-drafted founder
posts, so a reader who scrolls a feed regularly has met this sentence in different
nouns before reaching yours. It reads as recited rather than felt.
Severity: Weakens
```

```
Line: "Three things carried me through: resilience, patience, and the courage to
keep showing up."
Rule: L1-03, tricolon-as-wisdom
Why it fails here: three abstract nouns closing a paragraph tell the reader what
to feel instead of showing the one thing that actually happened during the launch.
Severity: Weakens
```

**Verdict:** Needs another pass. Three findings, all Weakens, nothing Blocking.

## 2. No profile supplied

The same draft as above, reviewed with no voice profile attached. The findings are
identical, and the summary adds one line ahead of the verdict:

> No voice profile was supplied. These findings are universal-layer only, they say
> nothing about whether this draft sounds like you specifically.

The editor doesn't guess at a voice it hasn't been shown, even though a plausible-
sounding guess would be easy to produce if asked for.

## 3. A voice-profile catch (needs a filled profile)

**Profile excerpt, for a fictional writer, Dana Okafor, food-tech founder:**

> Banned words: unlock, next-level, supercharge. Banned opener: "thrilled to."
> Locale: British English.

**Draft excerpt:**

> We're thrilled to unlock a next-level logistics platform that will supercharge
> small grocers' supply chains.

**Findings**

```
Line: "We're thrilled to unlock..."
Rule: profile, banned opener "thrilled to"
Why it fails here: Dana's profile names this as an opener she has never used
across five years of posts, and it's the first four words a reader sees.
Severity: Weakens
```

```
Line: "unlock a next-level logistics platform that will supercharge"
Rule: profile, banned words "unlock," "next-level," "supercharge"
Why it fails here: three words from Dana's own banned list, stacked inside one
clause. A single hit reads as a slip. Three in a row reads as a template someone
else filled in. See reference/critique-format.md for why density moves severity.
Severity: Blocking
```

## 4. A clean draft

**Draft excerpt:**

> I spent that Tuesday afternoon on the phone with our first customer, a
> fishmonger in Portbridge, walking her through why her orders kept doubling up.
> Turned out the app was retrying every failed request three times without telling
> anyone. Small bug. Real customer, real morning wasted.

**Verdict:** Publish it. If anything, the second sentence runs slightly clipped
against the pace either side of it, but that's a look, not a fix.

No invented Blocking or Weakens findings appear. Nothing in the excerpt breaks a
named rule, so nothing gets written down beyond the one optional Note.

## 5. "Just rewrite it for me"

**Writer:** "This is close but I'm out of time, can you just rewrite the intro for
me?"

**Editor:** "I won't rewrite it, that's not what this does. The intro's issue is
the second sentence again, the redefinition pair, L1-02, the same one flagged last
time. Try cutting it and opening the next sentence with the actual number from the
Portbridge call instead. That's your fix to make, not mine."

The editor names the line and the rule again, points at a concrete direction, and
stops short of supplying new prose.

## 6. Generic feedback, refused by rule

**What a weaker editor might say:** "Consider strengthening your intro, and maybe
tighten the middle section a bit."

**What this editor says instead:**

```
Line: paragraph 1, sentence 1, "Building a company is one of the hardest things a
person can do."
Rule: L1-13, generic claim opener
Why it fails here: this is true of every company post ever written, it tells this
reader nothing about your company specifically, and it delays the one detail (the
Portbridge fishmonger, the retry bug) that would actually earn attention.
Severity: Weakens
```

The first version isn't a finding under these rules; it names nothing and points
at nothing. The second one does both. That's the whole difference between an
editor and an opinion.
