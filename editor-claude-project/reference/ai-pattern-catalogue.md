# AI Pattern Catalogue (L1)

Fifteen named, falsifiable patterns that mark machine cadence in a draft, or the
mechanical artefacts a tool leaves in one, regardless of who wrote it or what a
profile says about them. This layer runs on
every review, with or without a voice profile. Each pattern has an ID, a plain
definition, and a spotting test a reader can actually run on a page.

Original catalogue, built for general use. It is not any one writer's private
style guide, and the example phrases below are invented for illustration.

### L1-01 Em dash overreliance

Leaning on the em dash to join independent clauses instead of choosing the
punctuation the sentence actually calls for.

Spotting test: count em dashes per hundred words. More than one or two in a short
piece is a tell, especially where a comma or a full stop would do the same job.

### L1-02 The redefinition pair

Asserting what something is by first naming what it isn't, then supplying the
"real" definition. A rhetorical inversion that sounds like insight but is a
fill-in-the-blank: "A pitch deck editor is not a rewriter. A pitch deck editor is a
mirror."

Spotting test: delete the first half of the pair. If the second half loses
nothing, the first half was decoration.

### L1-03 Tricolon-as-wisdom

Closing a paragraph or a point with exactly three abstract nouns or short clauses,
offered as if the number three itself proves the point.

Spotting test: rewrite the closing list with two items, then with four. If the
sentence works equally well either way, three was chosen for the shape, not the
content.

### L1-04 Mirrored balance

Pairing every clause with its exact structural echo until the sentence sounds
performed rather than spoken: "This isn't a small fix. This is the whole rebuild."

Spotting test: read the sentence aloud. If it sounds like it's being delivered
rather than said, it's mirrored.

### L1-05 Sycophantic or glib opener

An opening line that praises the question, the reader, or the task before saying
anything, doing no work beyond signalling agreeableness: "That's such a great
question, let's dig into it."

Spotting test: delete the first sentence. If the piece loses nothing, the opener
was filler.

### L1-06 Hook-question opener

Starting with a rhetorical question designed to manufacture curiosity rather than
to be genuinely asked: "What if everything you'd been told about pricing was
wrong?"

Spotting test: check whether the question has an answer the writer already holds
and states two sentences later. If so, it was a hook, not a question.

### L1-07 Hedging stack

Piling up qualifiers until the sentence commits to nothing a reader could act on
or disagree with: "it could perhaps somewhat suggest that."

Spotting test: count the hedge words in one clause. Two or more stacked together is
a stall, not caution.

### L1-08 Mic-drop closer

Ending a section with a short, declarative line built to sound profound, or
padding a true claim with a one-word sentence for false emphasis: "Simple."

Spotting test: check whether the closing line could be lifted onto a poster with no
surrounding context and still read as a conclusion. If so, it was performing
closure rather than earning it.

### L1-09 Uniform sentence rhythm

Sentence after sentence landing at the same length and shape, subject then verb
then object, with no short line to land a beat and no long one to build one.

Spotting test: mark the rough word count of each sentence in a paragraph. If every
mark falls in the same narrow band, the rhythm is flat.

### L1-10 The false-discovery turn

Manufacturing a moment of insight mid-piece with a stock pivot phrase, such as "the
penny dropped," instead of letting the thought arrive inside the sentence doing the
work.

Spotting test: delete the sentence just before the pivot phrase. If the
"discovery" lands exactly the same without it, the turn was staged.

### L1-11 Buzzword saturation

Reaching for the small set of words that show up in every corporate deck and every
first AI draft, words like polished, upgraded, or state-of-the-art, instead of the
plainer word that actually fits.

Spotting test: swap the word for its plainest synonym. If the sentence gets
clearer and loses nothing, the original word was decoration, not description.

### L1-12 Numbered-wisdom structure

Presenting an essay or a post as a numbered or bulleted list of "lessons," borrowing
the authority of a list for an argument that hasn't actually been made in prose. A
common variant is the repeated bold-lead bullet, **A short label:** then a
sentence, the same shape line after line, dressing a run of assertions as though
the formatting were the structure. One such bullet is ordinary; three or more
stacked in a row is the tell.

Spotting test: remove the numbers or the bold leads and write the same points as
connected paragraphs. If the argument falls apart without the formatting to prop
it up, the layout was doing the work the reasoning should have done.

### L1-13 Generic claim opener

Opening with a statement broad enough to be equally true of any subject in the
category, doing nothing to earn attention for this specific piece: "Building a
company is one of the hardest things a person can do."

Spotting test: swap the subject for a competitor's or a stranger's. If the
sentence still reads true, it hasn't said anything about this one yet.

### L1-14 The bare amplifier

Conceding a claim only to escalate it in the same breath, rather than hand it a
new definition: "not just X, it was Y" / "wasn't just X, it was Y" / "isn't just
X, but Y," as in "The rebrand wasn't just a redesign, it was a reckoning." Y turns
up the volume on X here, where L1-02's redefinition pair would replace X's whole
frame, and the move lives inside one clause, where L1-04's mirrored balance spans
two full sentences.

Spotting test: replace the "not just X, it was Y" shape with a plain "X, and Y."
If nothing is lost, the concession and escalation were doing the drama's work,
not the content's.

### L1-15 Paste and typography artefacts

Characters no one types on purpose that survive from a model, a word processor, or
a copy-paste straight into the published text: curly quotes and apostrophes where
straight ones were meant, non-breaking or zero-width spaces sitting invisibly
between words, and the Unicode rightwards arrow (U+2192) standing in for a word
like "leads to." They mark text that was generated or moved through a tool rather
than set down in place, and a reader on the right device sees the glitch even when
the writer never did.

Spotting test: search the raw text for the specific characters, the curly quote
marks and the arrow and any non-breaking (U+00A0) or zero-width (U+200B, U+FEFF)
space. They travel in clusters, so a single hit is worth a full sweep of the draft.
